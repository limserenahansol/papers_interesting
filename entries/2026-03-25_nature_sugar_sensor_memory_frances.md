## Paper in the PDF (verified from manuscript)

**Title:** Aversive learning hijacks a brain sugar sensor to consolidate memory  

**Authors:** Raquel Francés, Typhaine Comyn, Coraline Desnous, Francesca Bettoni, Alice Pavlowsky, Thomas Preat, Pierre-Yves Plaçais  

**Journal / ID:** *Nature* — article id `s41586-026-10306-z` (2026).  

**Model:** *Drosophila* (Pavlovian olfactory conditioning with shock; appetitive sucrose task in fasted flies).

### Summary (English)

Internal nutrient sensors are known to shape feeding and food-cued memory. This study shows that a **brain fructose-sensing circuit** (Gr43a-expressing neurons) is **co-opted for consolidation of aversive long-term memory (LTM)** even when animals are **satiated** and the task is **not food-related**. **Spaced** (but not massed) aversive training puts four Gr43a neurons per hemisphere into a **transient “disinhibited,” fasted-like state** that restores their ability to respond to ingested sugar despite satiation (normally Gr43a sensitivity is shut off by tachykinin from upstream dFB neurons). **After learning, eating sugar** activates these neurons and, via **thyrostimulin** release, **drives LTM consolidation**—parallel to the mechanism in **appetitive** sucrose learning. The same reset also **biases behavior** toward higher sucrose preference and intake (framed as a circuit basis for **non-homeostatic / “emotional eating”**-like coupling between internal state and feeding). Together: **spacing recruits a nutrient-sensor pathway that is usually feeding-linked to consolidate emotionally salient (aversive) memory.**
